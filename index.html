HAYAO MIYAZAKI

One of the best animators of all time

Here are some interesting facts about Hayao Miyazaki's life

Miyazaki was a huge fan of aviation

The theme of flying, sometimes on the most fantastic aircrafts, has been crucial in Miyazaki’s every work — starting with "Laputa: The Castle in the Sky," finding its place in "Porco Rosso" and "Howl's Moving Castle", and culminating in the director’s latest work "The Wind Rises."

He did not work within his specialty for a single day

Miyazaki has repeatedly admitted that he had dreamt of becoming an animator from childhood. He realized that in high school when he saw the animated film "The Legend of the White Snake" in the cinema.

He wasn’t going to become a director

A post-apocalyptic story about a heroic princess defending a peaceful valley from war for exhausted natural resources was published in Animage Magazine. The original agreement with publishers forbade Miyazaki to make a film version of "Nausicaä," but the popularity of the manga forced them to ask the animator to make a series of the same name. Miyazaki would agree only to a full-length film.The producer of Studio Ghibli, Toshio Suzuki, persuaded him to take up the next film — "Laputa: The Castle in the Sky." But despite the "prestigious" status of a director, Hayao Miyazaki has always been primarily an animation artist.

Nature plays a distinctive role in his films

Miyazaki himself admitted that the manner of depicting nature is one of the distinguishing features of the animated films created in Studio Ghibli.Miyazaki has always loved travelling. In his numerous trips around the world, he sought, above all, the scenery for his films. From Wales, for example, he brought ideas for "Laputa: The Castle in the Sky" and "Howl's Moving Castle" (based on a novel by British author Diana Wynne Jones). It’s interesting that Miyazaki "spied" on many landscapes from "Howl's Moving Castle" while traveling in Kazakhstan and Alsace.

He taught children to dream again

Speaking of his work, Hayao Miyazaki has repeatedly admitted that his own studio has finally allowed him to start making films that he thought were so necessary for his viewers. He wanted to teach children to imagine, dream of traveling and believe in miracles. The unconditional success of his fabulous parables proves that he succeeded in it.

" You must see with eyes unclouded by hate. See the good in that which is evil, and the evil in that which is good. Pledge yourself to neither side, but vow instead to preserve the balance that exists between the two. " --Hayao Miyazaki
